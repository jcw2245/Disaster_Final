FEMA is facing changes as hurricane season is approaching

The Federal Emergency Management Agency has faced several changes, which include the firing of more than 200 employees, while they are preparing for hurricane season, and many other storms.

By Jaylynn Ward

FEMA has undergone several changes from the new administration that could hinder the response to disasters in the future months. This is because of the mass firings and possible policy changes from this administration.

This agency, under the Department of Homeland Security, suffered from the mass layoffs from the new administration in February. It lost more than 200 employees. These employees were responsible for helping in aid for the natural disasters that occur around the country. They have recently seen an influx in floods and wildfires, but are preparing for the spring and summer months when severe storms and hurricanes pick up.

Along with the firings, there are policy changes proposed that will affect people who may experience a natural disaster. This includes:

  • The increase of the amount of damages required to be eligible
  • Limiting federal funding that is shared with states in a disaster
  • Denying some declarations, like snowstorms
  • Elimination of some grant programs within FEMA

Multiple news articles have stated that the agency is not ready for hurricane season as it approaches. This is after reported claims of former employees saying the organization was already struggling to meet demands as they were understaffed.

The U.S. faces different natural disasters every year. According to FEMA's website, "FEMA's mission is to help before, during and after disasters." The agency's budget is decided by Congress yearly, but sometimes this budget changes. As they responded to the hurricanes Helene and Milton, Congress gave the agency $20 billion in funding, according to CNN. This spending is due to the services of helping individuals and states with resources that people need at any point during a natural disaster.

Methodology

For this article, the information was taken from the National Centers for Environmental Information tables. Once I scraped the tables for the U.S. data, I focused on different aspects like the amount of average money spent and what gets spent per natural disaster. I also focused on the amount of disasters that take over a billion dollars to fix. I thought these two in comparison to one another would provide a good visual to be able to grasp how many events happen and how much they cost, as a billion can be a hard concept to understand number-wise. The amount of events helps with this understanding.
